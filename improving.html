Introduction

Many existing websites have accessibility barriers that make using them difficult or impossible for people with disabilities. They may be as a result of little or no consideration for accessibility when the website was created or may have slowly developed barriers as new content was added. Addressing accessibility may be tackled as a standalone project or part of broader site maintenance activities. In either case there are approaches that help make the process more efficient and effective.

What is this document for?

This document provides you with information on what is important when seeking to improve the accessibility of a website. The related document on Implementation Planning may be useful as many of the topics discussed are relevant to improving accessibility for existing websites.

Who is it for?

This document is for project managers, project team leaders, website owners and managers, web developers, and small business owners. It is likely that you will be relatively new to accessibility.

Using this document

The document presents the information as a structured list of topics that you will need to consider. Information from prior topics are often required for subsequent activities so you may need to consider how you plan to approach them.

Determine Scope and Target

The motivations and pressures for your improvement project will likely influence your scope and target. For example, if customer feedback on your website suggests an accessibility barrier that prevents use, then you probably want to fix that first. Similarly, if you are legally required to meet a certain level of accessibility, then your target will be at least that level.

By specifying what websites or pages are to be assessed and to what standard you will be able to confine the scope to something that is achievable. There is also the opportunity to define a multi-tiered target with different dates for different areas of the website or conformance levels.

Key actions

  • Set a standard and conformance level.

    Most organizations use WCAG as a target for accessibility and specify what level of success criteria they would aim to meet. You may already have an organizational policy that specifies what standard and level you need to achieve.

  • Define what is to be evaluated.

    Your project may be a single website or a part of a larger site. You will need to carefully consider your timescales and available resources when determining the scope. Where you initially have limited resources focusing on key user journeys and pages may bring the most significant benefits.

Assess the Situation

The goal of assessing your website or pages is to identify the accessibility barriers and gather information in order to plan for their repair. Evaluation can focus on specific points to help guide your improvement plan, such as determining if a particular issue is widespread or isolated, or what the impact is on users with disabilities.

Rather than thoroughly evaluating every target page, you can focus on representative areas in order to obtain more valuable information with less effort. For example, templates, style sheets, and any shared components, such as navigation bars and footers, only need to be evaluated once.

Knowing where identified issues originate can help in your improvement planning. For example, if only a few data tables are missing necessary markup, then you might include adding table markup to quality assurance processes. however, if none of the tables are properly marked up, you would add data table markup to your education plan. If tables from one development team are done properly, but another teams are not, this helps further clarify where education is needed.

Many organizations hire accessibility specialists to evaluate their website. They will also be able to provide guidance on how to respond to the identified barriers. This can include general recommendations on approach and priorities, specific recommendations on solutions, and guidance on the knowledge and skills needed for the repairs.

Key actions

  • Fix any known barriers.

    In some cases you may already be aware of accessibility barriers based on user feedback. If easily fixed it may be best to repair these before going further. As well as brining early improvement to the website, if you are involving users in your evaluation known existing barriers may make it difficult to obtain feedback on other areas.

  • Evaluate using a standardized approach.

    Ensure that you are as consistent as possible in applying your evaluation methodology. This allows you to repeat the evaluation and compare the results. WCAG-EM is a comprehensive and structured approach to help determine how well a website conforms to WCAG.

  • Evaluate with real users.

    In addition to helping identify and prioritize accessibility barriers, watching people struggle to use your site can be a powerful motivator for your improvement project team.

  • Use appropriate evaluation and repair tools.

    Web accessibility evaluation tools are programs and online services that help check if a website meets accessibility guidelines. There are also tools that help repair accessibility barriers. Some evaluation tools can be configured to be more useful to your specific project. For example, once you figure out your scope, you can configure the tool to only evaluate those criteria and pages. This can speed up the evaluation and simplify the report.

  • Consider how authoring tools might introduce barriers.

    Authoring tools are software or services used to create or modify websites, such as web page editors and content management systems (CMS). Some authoring tools provide better accessibility support than others. You might be able to improve accessibility support in your authoring tool by:

    • Configuring your tools, for example, sometimes accessibility features in authoring tools are disabled by default and need to be turned on
    • Using plug-ins or other add-on software to extend the accessibility support of your tools
    • Upgrading your existing tools to the latest version, if they have better accessibility support
    • Purchasing different tools with better accessibility support

    For some content, such as video or PDF, there may be an involved process with a number of tools involved. You may need to consider each step of the process to identify where issues are introduced and where they may be best addressed.

Prioritize Solutions

Prioritizing repairs will make implementation more manageable and aims to bring benefit to users as soon as possible. Also, you may find that resource limitations mean that not all repairs can be made immediately. When considering prioritization, there are two important factors to determine:

  1. What is the impact on people with disabilities?

    Accessibility barriers are often identified by WCAG success criteria. Each criteria has a Level (A, AA, AAA) that helps determine the impact of a particular accessibility barrier. The actual impact depends on the context of the barrier and target audience. For example, poor contrast in the main content has a high impact, whereas in generic footers it may have low impact.

    Evaluating with users will also help determine the impact of accessibility any barriers.

  2. What is the effort required for repair?

    The time, cost, and skills to repair a barrier will vary greatly. For example, repairing an issue in the footer could require a simple change to the template that is automatically propagated throughout the website, or it could require changing every page manually.

    Running workshops with representatives from all teams can help you quickly determine possible solutions and what effort would be required to implement them.

Key actions

  • Prioritize by barriers

    You will generally want to prioritize high impact easy fixes first. This will provide the most improvement in the shortest time. Creating a graph of impact versus effort can help you quickly determine the overall priority of barriers. This approach has several advantages: it usually takes less time overall, and you get lots of improvement quickly.

  • Prioritize by area

    It is usually best to first repair those areas that have the greatest impact on user's experience. Identify key pages or journeys within the website. For example, the home page may be a primary entry point, purchasing a product and payment process may be an important function. Frequently-used, high-traffic, pages and functionality can be identified using analytics.

Review and Plan to Revisit

Once repairs are completed, it is helpful to review what has been achieved and what is still left to do. An an ongoing discipline of web development it is useful to know what worked and what didn't. Knowing what problems still exist will help planning ongoing maintenance. It may also be necessary to communicate known issues to your site visitors especially where you are able to provide suitable alternative approaches.

Continue to evaluate accessibility in updated pages to ensure that new barriers are not introduced. See the Ongoing monitoring section of the Evaluation Approaches for Specific Contexts document.

Key actions

  • Document any remaining issues

    Knowing what was fixed, how, and what outstanding issues remain can help subsequent maintenance or redesign activities. This will help maintain momentum for accessibility and reduce subsequent evaluation costs.

  • Update or create your accessibility conformance statement

    Your accessibility statement can be used to provide contact details for feedback, outline your commitment to accessibility, and provide information to help users optimize their experience. You might also want to summarize how your site is supportive of accessibility, and where you still have work to do. Following completion of any accessibility repairs it is important to review your accessibility statement and update it with any improvements made and known issues.

  • Consider setting a follow up date

    It is important that the accessibility of your website is regularly reviewed. If possible, at the end of your project set a date for when the accessibility of the website should be next reviewed. This may be part of a regular maintenance schedule or as part of an upcoming improvement or redesign project.

Next steps: Strategic Planning

Thinking about accessibility tactically will generally be less cost effective and efficient that considering it as an integral part of your web development strategies. There are several things you can do to help your organization create more accessible websites as standard.

  • Implementation Plan for Web Accessibility outlines how to integrate accessibility throughout your web development. Ensure that when redesigns or updates are planned, accessibility in included from the onset of the project. Accessibility is much less costly and time-consuming when implemented from the beginning of a project, rather than the end.
  • Ensure that you have an effective accessibility policy in place that specifies that your website meet or exceed legal requirements.
    • Developing Organizational Policies on Web Accessibility describes considerations when making simple or comprehensive policies for organizations.
    • Legal and Policy Factors in Developing a Web Accessibility Business Case for Your Organization helps determine what requirements apply to your organization.
    • International Policies Relating to Web Accessibility links to information on government policies relating to web accessibility in countries around the world.
