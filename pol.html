Introduction

An organizational policy on web accessibility defines your goals for the creation, management, and delivery of accessible websites and content for both internal and external audiences. An organizational policy may also address the accessibility of tools used within the organization to create and access web content.

Accessibility policies can be standalone or integrated into other website policies or related documents, such as brand guidelines, coding standards, or project management frameworks. Including accessibility as part of general policies helps accessibility to be considered as a core feature rather than an afterthought.

Your organizational policy may be an internal document and not be suitable for website visitors. To communicate your effort, consider publishing an external statement of intent and broad goals for accessibility.

To learn about how an organizational policy forms part of a broader strategic approach to accessibility, read Strategic Planning for Web Accessibility.

What is this document for?

This document guides your development of a web accessibility policy that works within the context of your specific environment.

Example of simple policy

ACME Inc. is committed to ensuring that its website is accessible to people with disabilities. All the pages on our website will meet W3C WAI's Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 2.0, Level AA conformance. Any issues should be reported to accessibility@acme.inc.

We recommend that you begin with a simple policy statement and consider the following topics to determine where to expand upon it.

Reference Standards

The W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) provides a set of standards relevant to various aspects of web accessibility. Referencing these standards in policy documents adds credibility and aligns with global web standards.

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.0

Accessibility requirements for web content, including text, images, audio-visual materials, and the code used for structure, style, and interactions.

WCAG is applicable for any organization with a website, as it addresses the creation and maintenance of web content. WCAG is also relevant for content delivered using mobile specific websites or mobile applications.

Find out more at WCAG Overview

Authoring Tool Accessibility Guidelines (ATAG)

Accessibility requirements for web authoring tools, including software and services that are used to produce web content, for example HTML editors, content management systems (CMS), or online forums. ATAG guidelines outline both how to make the tools accessible and also how the tools can be built to help create more accessible content.

ATAG is applicable for websites that provide users the opportunity to generate content, such as adding comments, posting to forums, or uploading images or videos. If your organization provides tools, such as CMS's, for staff or customers to manage websites and content, then ATAG is also relevant.

Find out more at ATAG Overview

User Agent Accessibility Guidelines (UAAG)

Accessibility requirements for web browsers, media players, and some assistive technologies that process and present web content to users.

UAAG is applicable when additional plug-ins, such as media players, are provided to deliver content or when custom controls are developed to provide non-standard functionality. Where mobile applications deliver web content as part of the application, UAAG may also be relevant. If your organization provides browsers for staff, then UAAG may be relevant as part of the procurement process.

Find out more at ATAG Overview

Version number

Note that you may want to reference W3C standards without specifying a version number. Doing so means you can always be certain of referring to the most up-to-date standards. Alternatively, referencing a specific version will allow you to review new standards and deliberately plan required changes. Referencing is explained in Referencing Guidelines and Other Technical Specifications.

Define Conformance Levels

W3C WAI guidelines provide three levels of conformance: A, AA, and AAA. The generally accepted level of conformance in many countries is Level AA. Selecting a conformance level that is realistically achievable for your particular organization is critical for the success of your policy.

What to include

  • For each referenced standard, specify what level of conformance is to be achieved.

Examples

Simple example of referencing WCAG

ACME Inc. seeks to make sure our website conforms to W3C WAI WCAG Level AA.

Example of reference to WCAG and ATAG

At ACME Inc. we seek to ensure all of our websites and web applications, both customer-facing and for internal use, conform to all Level AA success criteria of W3C WAI WCAG 2.0. We also aim to ensure that our authoring tools conform to all Level AA criteria of ATAG.

Define Scope of Policy

Your organizational policy should clearly identify what website content it will cover. Content available via an intranet or other internal systems should also be considered. For all identified content consider how new, updated, and existing web content will be treated. Also consider what standards compliance you require for any content that is provided by third-parties.

Policies can also include the tools used to produce the web content. Doing so can help you achieve better website accessibility and better support staff with disabilities. Additionally, considering the browsers and other tools used within the organization can help address organizational accessibility issues.

What to include

  • Clear list of web content the policy will cover.
  • List of web content provided internally that will covered by the policy.

Other considerations

  • Where you have legacy content, consider how the policy covers new, updated, and existing content.
  • If you have web content or code supplied by third-parties, consider how the policy applies to them.
  • Internal tools and browsers can impact both the accessibility of created content and the ability of staff with disabilities to do their job.

Examples

Simple example of content scope

This policy applies to all new, updated, and existing web content on http://www.acme.inc/ and all content provided internally at http://intranet.acme.inc/.

Example including third-party content scope

This policy applies to all web content produced or updated by ACME Inc. In addition, ACME Inc. will ensure third-party content providers are aware of our web accessibility policy. ACME Inc. will also favor providers based on their accessibility conformance claims.

Set Conformance Milestones

For all items in scope, define a date by which it will meet the policy, or if it already meets the policy, when it was last reviewed. Deadlines should be realistic but issues should not be left too long.

In some cases, a phased approach might be appropriate by first rapidly addressing significant accessibility barriers, then implementing fixes for other issues, for example, as part of a planned maintenance update. Clearly outline any details of this type of approach in your policy.

Information on factors to consider when prioritizing issues is available in Improving the Accessibility of Your Website.

What to include

  • Dates for when the policy goals will be met.

Other considerations

  • Consider whether to define a staged approach to fix significant accessibility barriers more quickly.
  • Consider prioritizing important features and functions.

Examples

Simple example of conformance milestone

By February 1, 2015, ACME Inc's web content will meet WCAG 2.0, Level AA conformance.

Example of staged conformance milestones

By November 1, 2014, ACME Inc's websites will meet WCAG 2.0, Level A conformance; and by May 1, 2015 web content will meet WCAG 2.0, Level AA conformance.

Example of supplier milestone

By June 1, 2015, all vendors of content management systems (CMS) used by ACME Inc's should provide information regarding their plans for ATAG 2.0 conformance in future versions of their software. By June 1, 2016 ACME Inc. will preferentially purchase ATAG conformant authoring tools.

Define Monitoring and Review Process

Progress towards policy goals should be reviewed regularly. Any changes in timescale or milestones that have been met should be updated in the policy.

Specify a recommended process and schedule for reviewing all web content and tools in scope to ensure that the target level of accessibility is maintained.

Capturing feedback from users who might have accessibility difficulties can help you gather information on barriers. Your policy should include information on what mechanisms will be included and how feedback is to be handled and responded to.

What to include

  • Specify a regular review period and process.
  • Outline how user feedback will be captured, responded to, and used.

Examples

Example simple review policy

All areas of the ACME Inc. website will be reviewed annually using the process described at Website Accessibility Conformance Evaluation Methodology. Reviews are the responsibility of the IT department. Accessibility checks will be incorporated into the publishing workflow for all new content.

Example of feedback policy

Each page of the website will include a link to a form allowing users to submit feedback on the site; this information will be compiled and considered during the review process. Any submitted feedback will be responded to within 48 hours.

Example Policies

Example of simple policy

ACME Inc. is committed to ensuring the accessibility of its web content to people with disabilities. All of the content on our website will meet W3C WAI's Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 2.0, Level AA conformance. Any issues should be reported to accessibility@acme.inc.

Example of in-depth policy

ACME Inc. is committed to ensuring the accessibility of its web content to people with disabilities. New and updated web content produced by our organization will meet W3C WAI's Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 2.0, Level A conformance, by February 1, 2015.

Existing web content produced by our organization, and new, updated, and existing web content provided for our site by third-party developers, will meet Level AA conformance by November 1, 2015.

We will initiate an internal monitoring program by June 1, 2015. Vendors supplying software used to develop our site will be required to provide information by June 1, 2016, on conformance to W3C WAI's Authoring Tool Accessibility Guidelines 2.0, Conformance Level AA.

Our home page and our 'about this site' page will include a link to a public version of this policy.

All of our web pages will include a link to our 'Feedback' form. The form will include an option to indicate the feedback is related to an accessibility barrier or issue. Any submitted feedback will be responded to by our customer services team within 48 hours. Feedback related to accessibility issues will be passed to our Web Management Team for assessment.

We will review this policy in the future to consider updating it to reference later versions of W3C's accessibility guidelines.

Next steps: Maintaining your policy

If your policy includes milestones and references to particular standards by version number, then review the policy regularly to ensure that it is accurate and up-to-date. Any review should check if milestones have been met, as well as checking if any changes are required due to new or updated standards.

What to consider

  • Identify who is responsible for maintaining the policy.
  • Specify a regular review period.

Long term: Strategic Planning

An accessibility policy is one part of a broader strategic approach to accessibility. Making accessibility an integral part of your web development strategy is more cost effective and efficient than considering it in isolation. There are several things you can do to help your organization create more accessible websites as standard.

Strategic Planning for Web Accessibility outlines how to integrate accessibility throughout your web development. Ensure that when redesigns or updates are planned, accessibility is included from the start of the project. Accessibility is much less costly and time-consuming when tackled at the beginning of a project, rather than the end.
