Introduction

Incorporating accessibility into your project or organization is as straight forward as any other planning task. This document provides you with guidance, questions, and a framework for how to approach planning for accessibility. Your actual approach will vary depending the size of your organization, what you are aiming to achieve, and where you are in the development process.

Additional guidance is available if you are looking at Improving the Accessibility of Your Website.

This document is for project managers, project team leaders, and small business owners. It is also useful for accessibility consultants who are engaged in supporting a project or broader organizational change.

The following summarizes what this document presents. This does not suggest a fixed order and should be used however best suits your project and organization.

  • Determine Goal and Promote Buy-In - identifies important themes to address regarding organizational awareness and buy-in for accessibility.
  • Establish Roles and Responsibilities - discusses assigning important accessibility responsibilities and what these entail.
  • Develop Organizational Policy - outlines the importance of a policy and how it can be used.
  • Review Available Tools and Resources - outlines questions important for understanding accessibility of resources used in developing new websites.
  • Assess Skills and Training - explore what skills your team have and what additional training might be required.
  • Integrate into Project Life Cycle - discusses what activities are relevant by project stage.
  • Promote Organizational Awareness - explores what your project legacy can bring to improving overall organizational accessibility.
  • Maintain and Monitor - what actions and activities are important as part of ongoing website accessibility.

Determine Goal and Promote Buy-In

Consider your organization's current approach, understanding, and view of accessibility. Ensure buy-in from your organization to help in securing budget and time for accessibility activities. Undertake activities to convince stakeholders or executives of the value of an accessibility lead approach.

Key actions

  • Determine and document organizational or project driver for accessibility.
  • Identify and document the project goal with regards to accessibility.
  • Explore the limitations of your influence to enact change or extract budget.
  • Ensure stakeholder buy-in for the goal.
  • If required, prepare a business case to secure stakeholder backing.

Discussion

Driver for accessibility

Understanding why you and your organization are seeking to incorporate accessibility can be important when there are clashes in project or organizational priorities. It also helps you to identify activities important to the success of the project. For example, if senior management have indicated that all new websites must be accessible, then there is a need to ensure that the accessibility of the website is reported to them regularly. If you are responding to national legislation it may be beneficial to convince your project team and stakeholders that accessibility is something more than simply a legal burden or it may be appropriate to seek external validation to the websites accessibility claims.

The rationale for accessibility should be recorded as part of the broader scope of your planning. This makes sure that everyone is clear why accessibility is important within this particular project. It also ensures that any further developments to the website include the same accessibility considerations.

Identify your goal

Knowing what you are aiming to achieve helps you make decisions about what activities are necessary and appropriate. It could be that you are seeking to bring about more accessibility thinking within all aspects of your organization. Perhaps you are a project manager seeking to understand what activities and questions are important to plan for when creating a new accessible web service. Or you may be part of a small organization seeking to ensure that the new website you are commissioning is as accessible as possible.

For example, if you are seeking to bring about organizational change then there will be a need to plan broader activities such as reviewing purchasing procedures, recruitment criteria, and training provision. If your goal is only to create a WCAG 2.0 AA compliant website then it will be important to undertake an audit, to these guidelines, possibly supported by accessibility testing.

As with the driver for accessibility, the goal should be recorded in the scope of your planning.

Organizational influence

What influence you have within your organization is a significant factor in what can be achieved. As project manager you many not have much influence, but you may have the banking of senior management who see your activities as the first steps in organizational change. In this case you will need to consider what upward reporting is required and how to manage project legacy, such as changes in organizational design guidelines, procurement process, and content management system recommendations.

It could also be that your organization has not yet embraced accessibility and you are keen to promote the value of such an approach. In this case activities such as setting out a business case to stakeholders will be of importance, particularly if there is a need to secure additional project funds.

Promote Buy-In

Stakeholder and executive buy-in is an extremely important part in achieving your aims. Required promotional activities will depend on your driver for accessibility and what influence you have with stakeholders. The ideal situation is that the driver is concrete and accepted, for example, national legislation, and the executive level of the organization is actively backing accessibility. In this case it may be sufficient to identify the necessary activities and incorporate within the project budget.

More likely there are factors that will limit what can be done at the project level. In this case there may be a need to begin by creating a business case for accessibility and actively promoting it with the stakeholders and executives. The aim is to encourage budget controllers to be responsive to extending project budget for accessibility related activities.

Depending on your goal, it may also be necessary to promote accessibility more broadly within the organization. This is particularly important where the goal is to affect change for subsequent projects. This may require presentations and information being made available to people and departments beyond the project team and immediate stakeholders.

Establish Roles and Responsibilities

Identify and appoint individuals who are responsible for accessibility within your project team or organization, and outline what that role entails.

Key actions

  • Identify roles and assign responsibilities across different areas of the project, such as design, content creation, development, and quality assurance.
  • Consider what organizational areas might impact on the accessibility of the project, such as human resources, procurement, marketing, organizational executive, legal, and ensure they are aware of their role and responsibilities.
  • Communicate up and down within the project and organization who the responsible parties are.
  • If you are using an external company or organization, ensure that they are aware of your accessibility requirements and of their responsibilities.
  • For websites and projects spanning multiple jurisdictional regions, ensure that responsibility is assigned for each region.

Discussion

Identifying and appointing individuals responsible for accessibility is an important part of ensuring that accessibility is considered throughout the design and development process, and across the organization. The assigned roles ensure that any accessibility related issues are not neglected and that everyone on the team knows where to take accessibility questions.

e.g.

Example responsibilities

  • Marketing - Ensuring good accessibility practice within the brand or design guidelines.
  • Quality assurance - Test for and track accessibility issues within the code.
  • Development - Maintain code libraries with accessible components.
  • Purchasing - Ensure that organizational accessibility policy is incorporated within procurement processes.
  • Acceptance testing - Ensure that any agency delivered website or component meets your desired level of accessibility.
  • Recruitment - Incorporate accessibility skills within recruitment profiles.

Responsibility may be spread throughout your project team, particularly in larger teams. For example, the responsibility for ensuring accessible visual design may be shared by the design manager and digital designer. In other situations, one individual may have multiple responsibilities. For example, in a smaller team a developer may be responsible for the coding as well as for the quality assurance testing. What is important, is to identify the necessary responsibilities and assign them to appropriate individuals.

While some roles and responsibilities are focused on the immediate project needs, others are focused on ensuring sustainability of the necessary frameworks that facilitate accessibility. For example, part of the responsibility of the role for 'accessible design' would be to ensure that members of the design team have the appropriate skills and knowledge to deliver accessible designs. Other areas that you may also need to consider include human resources, procurement, marketing, organizational executive, and legal. Identified responsible parties in these areas have the task of incorporating accessibility into existing policies, practices, and procedures.

For some organizations the task of creating a website, or part of it, such as the visual design, is often placed in the hands of an external company. In this case you, as the purchaser, will still hold responsibility for website accessibility. It is important to ensure that you are clearly communicating what you expect in terms of accessibility to the supplier.

The following list provides examples of other functions that might be important to consider when assigning responsibility:

  • Sign-off process and authority for accessibility of deliverables.
  • Manage and procure accessibility audits and testing.
  • Executive presentations of progress and business value of accessibility.
  • Regular communication between regional and departmental accessibility teams.
  • Ongoing review and sharing of new accessibility techniques.
  • Identify and respond to accessibility training needs.

Develop Accessibility Policy

Create or review your organizational or project accessibility policy. This should outline your commitment to accessibility, relevant standards or legislation, and contact information. There is likely to be an internal and external version of this document.

Key actions

  • Identify and review any existing policy.
  • Establish an organizational policy on web accessibility.
  • Communicate organizational policy.
  • Review policies and procedures to incorporate organizational policy.

Discussion

Your organizational policy captures and communicates your intentions to create accessible websites and services. Developing Organizational Policies provides information on how to develop your policy, this discussion concentrates on what the policy is for and what to do with it.

Existing policy

An organizational policy may already exist or it may be that there is a general statement on website accessibility conformance. In larger organizations other departments, such as online development teams, corporate social responsibility, and user experience, may be developing or have developed accessibility policies. If such a policy exists it may be that it supports your accessibility goals or does not encompass everything you are hoping to achieve. In either case it is worth asking some questions. How is the policy implemented? Are there any issues implementing it? Is it up to date?

Communicating your policy

Making people, both internal and external, aware of the existence of your policy is an important step in acknowledging your intent. Communication is also important for subsequent projects. The more the policy is promoted, the greater the awareness of it within your organization. For external audiences you may need a policy written in a non-technical way, without reference to internal departments or processes. Internal audiences will require more information regarding responsible parties, contacts, and on the technical elements of website accessibility. It may also be beneficial to provide a facility and policy for readers to feedback with compliments or concerns.

Updating processes and procedures

Where your goals have an element of organizational change there will be a need to explore how the policy is incorporated into other organizational processes and procedures. For example, your procurement processes would outline how externally purchased websites or elements should meet your accessibility policy and what acceptance criteria are acceptable. Policy and procedural review can be an extensive activity that may form a project by itself.

Smaller organizations

For smaller organizations the policy may be a simple statement of intent and reference to WCAG 2.0 as an appropriate international standard. Communicating this may only require publishing it on your website and ensuring the organization is aware of it.

Related resources

  • Developing Organizational Policies on Web Accessibility - Describes considerations when making simple or comprehensive policies for organizations.

Review Available Tools and Resources

Identify and review what internal design and development resources are available and how they already support the project goals and accessibility policy. Resources could include design guidelines, templates, coding standards, coding libraries, content management systems, and content strategies. You may also be able to research common practices in other organizational websites to identify common accessible solutions and barriers.

Key actions

  • Identify and review authoring tools.
  • Locate and review any accessibility evaluations of existing websites or online services.
  • Review any brand or design guidelines.
  • Review any coding libraries or standards.
  • Assign staff to address any identified issues with organizational shared tools and resources.

Discussion

When planning the creation of a new website you will need to consider what standard organizational resources are available and how accessible these are. If you are looking to improve organization wide accessibility, reviewing and improving resources that all projects use is a great way to bring about extensive change.

Existing websites

For some organizations there may already be many websites or services. If planning for a new website within a similar framework or style it may be useful to understand what solutions and problems currently exist. For organizational change evaluating existing websites will be an important starting point in understanding the extent of work required to address online accessibility issues. It may be that accessibility evaluations already exist for some of your organizations websites. Reviewing these reports can be more cost effective than commissioning new ones. It is important in this case to ensure that the evaluation was performed to a similar standard as any new or updated accessibility policy.

Authoring tools

The most commonly known authoring tool is the Content Management System (CMS) but there may be other authoring tools used in creating content for your website. For example, the creation of PDF documents will involve a number of tools or a defined process. These tools and processes need to be reviewed in order to ensure that the final document is accessible.

Most websites are managed via some form of CMS. This allows content managers to organize, edit and publish website content. There are two aspects of the CMS that need to be reviewed: how accessible is the tool itself, and how does the tool help promote the creation of more accessible content. The former is important for any staff members with disabilities who create content for the website.

You may find that your organization subscribes to a particular CMS that has known accessibility barriers that are beyond the scope of your project. Reporting these issues to the CMS provider is important in order to encourage them to produce accessible solutions. How you resolve these issues in your project largely depends on the nature of the issue. For those concerned with how the CMS supports users with disabilities, it may be that additional training or changes in processes are required. For issues that result in the create of inaccessible components it may that your development team can create bespoke solutions that mitigate the issue.

Any video production process should also be reviewed in order to ensure that the final video content is produced in a manner as to promote accessibility. For example, does the process account for the time to create the transcript and captions?

Design guidelines and coding libraries

Your organization may have design or branding guidelines, content guidelines, or may have standard coding libraries or guidelines for online services. Ideally when all these assets were created they were assessed for accessibility. If this is not the case and these resources must be used in your project, then it will benefit you to assess them for accessibility. Reviewing shared resources such as these will have a broad reaching benefit in your organization as any subsequent projects will benefit from the improved accessibility in core resources.

Planning for reviews and resolutions

If there is a need to review existing websites, organizational resources, authoring tools, or even previous evaluations, then this will need to be factored in early in the project plan to allow findings to be addressed in a timely fashion.

For organizational resources, such as design guidelines, it is likely that there is an involved sign off process to follow when changes are requested. Identifying and communicating with the owner of these resources will allow you to understand any constraints. This is also an opportunity to obtain additional budget or staff as maintenance and improvement of the resource may fall within another departmental remit.

Finally, you will also need to ensure that any findings and action are captured in a way that they will available during the appropriate phase of the project.

The section on Project Life Cycle discusses the evaluation activities in slightly more depth.

Assess Skills and Training

Assess expertise and motivation of design, development and content personnel. Identify and plan for training key personnel within the project or organization. Secure external training resources to create and/or deliver training options. Consider what additional recruitment needs might be required and how recruitment might change to respond to your organizational policy.

Key actions

  • Identify key personnel responsible for the design, development, and content for the project.
  • Evaluate motivation and understanding of accessibility issues.
  • Evaluate accessibility expertise.
  • Budget and plan in training for team members.
  • If recruitment is necessary, ensure recruitment briefs include accessibility requirements.
  • If seeking organizational change, review and update recruitment criteria.
  • If using external suppliers, evaluate their ability to provide accessible solutions

Discussion

The ability to create an accessible website is larger dependent on the skills and expertise of the team of designers, developers, and content creators. The aim is to develop a better understanding of the need and value of accessibility, as well as improving the skills of key personnel.

Identifying personnel

Identifying those individuals whose skills will be important to the success of the project should be a straight forward matter as they are already likely to be identified as the assigned project resources. If there is a need to recruit additional personnel for the project, then accessibility awareness and expertise should form part of the recruitment brief. New recruits can be an important resource in quickly developing internal accessibility expertise. For larger teams it may be that there are key personnel who will have specific responsibility for accessibility, although all team members will need a good general awareness.

Evaluate awareness

Once you have identified important personnel, you will need to carry out an initial evaluation of their motivation for and understanding of accessibility. This can be achieved using surveys or interviews. Knowing this information will allow you to plan activities designed to address misconceptions and build understanding. This is important when changing broad organizational attitudes towards accessibility and developing awareness as to why it is important. Where there is a more contained project team it is valuable to know what personnel consider to be accessibility to be and what specific gaps in understanding might exist. Knowing this helps developing training resources designed to improve awareness and appreciation of the issues.

Evaluate expertise

Technical, design, and content creation staff will need to have both a good awareness of the need for accessible solutions and the technical expertise to deliver accessible solutions. Self-reporting of skills in this area can be adequate for an initial assessment. Again, the aim is to understand what training will be required.

External suppliers

When using external suppliers for any aspect of website creation it is important to know that are experienced in delivering accessible solutions. Ensure that you request credentials in this area and ideally check a sample of their deliverables.

Training

Once you have identified who requires additional training you will need to develop or otherwise source the appropriate training. There is help and information on how to structure training materials. What you need in terms of training depends largely on your goals, the organizational and team awareness, and the expertise in the team. Example training sessions might include:

  • Introduction to accessibility
  • Benefits of accessible websites
  • Writing accessible content
  • Visual design and accessibility
  • Accessible web development

If you have experienced accessibility professionals internally then they may be able to develop the required resources, possibly in collaboration with your training or HR teams.

Related resources

  • Developing Web Accessibility Presentations and Training - Helps you develop presentations and training on web accessibility.

Integrate into Project Life Cycle

Start early. Review each stage of the project considering where and what accessibility checks could be incorporated. Continually promote accessibility as a positive challenge within the team to encourage creative thinking of accessible solutions.

back to page contents

Promote Organizational Awareness

Consider how the project success or difficulties need to be feed to stakeholders, the wider organization and the website audiences. Plan for ongoing issue tracking of website, organizational assets, such as design guidelines, and policies and procedures.

back to page contents

Maintain and Monitor

Plan for regular content reviews and ensure the accessibility policy is incorporated into maintenance activities. Provide an appropriate feedback mechanism for users with accessibility issues.
